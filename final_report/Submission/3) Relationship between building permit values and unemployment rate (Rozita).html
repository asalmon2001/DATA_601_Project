Guiding Question: The Relationship Between Residential/Non-Residential Building Permit Values and Unemployment Rate¶

Domain¶

This study aims to investigate Calgary’s building permits in order to observe the influence the building permit values have on unemployment rate. Shelter is one of the fundamental needs of humans to survive. From the earliest era when homes were built with basic resources like branches or stones, mankind has since advanced to a point in which it can construct solid and sturdy buildings literally anywhere and designate them as residences, offices, schools, factories, and hospitals. Based on the new report regarding the housing crisis in Calgary which was published by the City of Calgary on Sep,2023 approximately 20% of Calgary households are facing difficulties affording where they currently live, equating to 80,000 households allocating over 30% of their income toward housing costs. Calgary's reputation as one of the world's most livable cities is being jeopardized by a combination of factors, including a shortage of available rental properties, rent hikes that surpass typical increases, and the continuous escalation of housing prices. My domain will be investigating the relationship between building permit values and the unemployment rate. The construction industry is vast and serves as a valuable gauge of the private sector's health. A decrease in the issuance of building permits can serve as an early warning sign of an impending economic slowdown. Such a slowdown can initially be subtle, reminiscent of the housing crisis in 2006, but it has the potential to escalate into a major crisis, akin to the Great Recession. During a recession, unemployment rates rise rapidly and often take a considerable amount of time to return to lower levels. This can result in costly and enduring consequences for the overall economy.

Dataset¶

Our primary datasets are Monthly Economic Indicators and Building Permits, both found on Open Calgary [Monthly Economic Indicators | Open Calgary , Building Permits | Open Calgary]. Each dataset contains many features that help facilitate the investigation of Calgary’s housing crisis and economic trends and lead us to our guiding questions. For my guiding question, I have used Monthly Economic Indicators dataset. The Monthly Economic Indicator dataset contains data about unemployment statistics, wages, building permit values, total housing, retail sales, and inflation rate from the 1970s to 2022. The columns related to unemployment include unemployment rates (Canada, Alberta, Calgary), Calgary total employment per 1000 persons, Calgary total employment change per 1000 persons (month-over-month and year-over-year), and Calgary average hourly wage. Columns related to building permit values are both, residential and non-residential building permit values, measured in dollars. Total housing is measured month-over-month and counts the number of residential housing projects started; there are two columns for total housing for projects within Calgary and projects in the Calgary Metropolitan Area (CMA). Finally, there are columns for retail sales (measured in billions of dollars) and inflation rate.

Data Wrangling: The Process¶

In the data preparation phase, we streamlined the dataset by removing superfluous columns, adjusting column datatypes, and omitting rows with missing values. We focused our analysis on the period from 2000 to 2023, ensuring the exclusion of any anomalies such as blanks, zero, or negative values for building permits. It was essential to convert the date entries into a standardized datetime format. For clarity and ease of reference, column names were updated.

For a more insightful analysis, we segmented the data yearly and summed up monthly building permit values to get the annual totals. Likewise, we computed the annual average from the monthly unemployment rates. Our visual representation employed line plots to elucidate data trends, with scatter plots superimposed to highlight the correlation between building permit values and unemployment rates.

It then employs the strptime method from the datetime module to convert this string into a datetime object. def dateobject(date):
        dateobject = datetime.datetime.strptime(str(date), "%Y-%m-%d")
        dateobject = dateobject.isoformat()
        return dateobject

Data Transformation¶

The provided code focuses on refining the dataset for clearer visualization by transitioning from monthly to annual data aggregation. Initially, the 'Date' column of the re_df dataframe is converted into a standard datetime format using the pd.to_datetime function. Subsequently, the data is grouped by year, and for each year, the sum of both 'Residential_Permit_Value' and 'NonResidential_Permit_Value' columns is computed. Additionally, the average 'Unemployment_Rate' for each year is calculated. The resulting dataframe, result, is then restructured to have the 'Date' (representing the year) as its index. This transformation simplifies the dataset, condensing monthly data points into annual summaries, which facilitates a more streamlined and interpretable visualization.

Conclusion¶

Calgary Building Permit Values and Unemployment: A Glimpse¶

Calgary's building permit values serve as a meaningful gauge to understand the city's economic health, and they're notably intertwined with unemployment trends. This relationship, although clear, is influenced by multifaceted factors including economic shifts, population growth, regulatory decisions, and overall consumer optimism.

Our data visualization reveals a clear inverse correlation: as building permit values rise, unemployment tends to decrease. This pattern, however, manifests differently across sectors. A surge in commercial building permits is typically a positive economic indicator, suggesting businesses are thriving and job opportunities are expanding. In contrast, an increase in residential building permits, especially for single-family homes, signals a broader economic confidence among residents, as more individuals find themselves in a position to purchase homes.

In essence, building permit values aren't just static numbers; they offer foresight. An upward trend suggests a vibrant economy with increased construction and business activities. Conversely, a decline could be an early sign of economic deceleration. By closely monitoring these metrics, we can anticipate the potential direction of Calgary's economic winds.
